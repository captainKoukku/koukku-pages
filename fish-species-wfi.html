Fish species: biology and weather sensitivity

Perch (ahven)

Perca fluviatilis — European perch

Luke — species guide (FI) Luontoportti (FI) FishBase

Feeding A diurnal, shoaling visual predator: strike efficiency peaks at intermediate light—not deep shade and not harsh glare (Helfman 1981; Diehl 1988). The profile centres the main feeding window around midday and late afternoon (Jamet & Lair 1991; Eriksson 1978).

Temperature Perch tolerate warmth better than most other profiled species: aquaculture work reports growth optima near 23 °C, while coastal wild recruitment is often strongest around 16–22 °C at the surface. Above ~28 °C fish retreat to cooler layers and feeding shuts down.

Reproduction Spring spawning in the shallows: females deposit adhesive egg ribbons on submerged plants or rocks; males follow and fertilise. Eggs are tough, but cannibalism among larvae can be severe in dense populations (culture studies).

WFI highlights A strong link to falling barometric pressure when feeding (percid pattern in Stoner 2004). No lunar term in the model (daylight sight-feeder; no peer-reviewed lunar feeding correlation in the profile notes). Cloud cover: broken sky (~10–50 %) supports visual hunting.

Pike (hauki)

Esox lucius — northern pike

Luke — species guide (FI) Luontoportti (FI) FishBase

Feeding Pike are textbook ambush predators: they use cover and the contrast between prey silhouette and light. Hard sun reduces success (prey often spot the shadow first); solid overcast softens activity slightly. Light-to-moderate wind and chop disorient prey and cut surface glare—both favour pike (Lehtonen & Niemelä 1986; Casselman & Lewis 1996).

Temperature Literature growth optima near 21–23 °C; feeding tapers when warm surface water exceeds ~25 °C. In winter and spring, feeding can stay steady from roughly 10 °C upward (telemetry studies).

Reproduction Spring flooded meadows and weedy shallows: females scatter batches of sticky eggs; males fertilise externally. Spawning sites are temperature- and oxygen-sensitive; vegetation gives fry cover.

WFI highlights Dawn and dusk crepuscular peaks (Baktoft et al. 2012). Six-hour pressure fall carries the heaviest weight in the registry among these species (Stoner 2004 — piscivores). Wind and gust factor matter; moon is modest (weak literature, strong angling lore).

Zander (kuha)

Sander lucioperca — pikeperch / zander

Luke — species guide (FI) Luontoportti (FI) FishBase

Feeding Zander are low-light specialists: large pupils and a tapetum lucidum yield at least twice pike retinal sensitivity under the same conditions (Horppila et al. 2018). Strikes concentrate below ~1 lux (Bergman 1991). The profile therefore favours cloudy skies and a strong moon term—moonlight extends the feeding window.

Behaviour Diel rhythm leans to evening, night, and around midnight: fish move up in the water column at dusk to hunt smelt, perch, and similar prey (Schulze et al. 2006). Calm water supports tight shoals; rough surface chop breaks them up (Pekcan-Hekim et al. 2010; Finnish catch-practice data cited in the profile).

Reproduction Spawning often on sand or gravel bars and basin margins in spring; males guard eggs. Population density and lake trophic state strongly affect larval survival.

WFI highlights Highest lunar weight of the major piscivores in the app; moderate solunar; falling pressure (percid signal, Stoner 2004). Water temperature optimum sits warmer than for salmonids.

Atlantic salmon (lohi)

Salmo salar — JSON label: salmon and lake salmon (lohi ja järvilohi)

Luke — species guide (FI) Luontoportti (FI) FishBase

Feeding Feeding in sea and river shifts with life stage. Upriver adults stress above ~20 °C; migration can stall (Solomon & Sambrook 2004; DFO). The profile stresses cool water (a lower optimum band than brown trout) and reduced surface light (Karppinen et al. 2002; sea-cage appetite work, Kadri et al. 1991).

Behaviour Dawn-skewed activity versus a secondary dusk peak (parr gut-fullness and cage studies). In coastal angling, light chop can bunch baitfish and mask lure entry (Lacroix & McCurdy 1996).

Reproduction Autumn redds in gravelly riverbeds: females excavate a pit, eggs are buried in gravel. Sea-run fish home to natal streams; landlocked strains adapt to lakes.

WFI highlights Six-hour pressure change is weak (Stoner 2004 found no strong salmonid barometric pattern). Moon and solunar are moderate (Vøllestad et al.; secondary migration cue in Solomon & Sambrook 2004). Profile note: the folk rule “rising pressure = salmon” lacks peer-reviewed support and is flagged for calibration.

Burbot (made)

Lota lota — burbot

Luke — species guide (FI) Luontoportti (FI) FishBase

Feeding Burbot are benthic and night-active: in summer, locomotor activity is roughly 180° out of phase with daylight—active at night; under polar-night winter, daytime activity can increase (Müller 1973). Shallow nocturnal excursions cluster at twilight (Harrison et al. 2013). Heavy cloud acts as a proxy for dimmer light reaching deep habitat.

Temperature Bioenergetic growth stalls above ~18 °C; thermal preferendum in the literature near 13 °C. Burbot feed and move in the cold—winter spawning under ice at 0–4 °C (Hofmann & Fischer 2002; Cott et al. 2013).

Reproduction Mid-winter spawning under ice; eggs develop slowly in cold, oxygen-rich water. Young stages are small benthic predators.

WFI highlights The app’s highest lunar weight (nocturnal biology). Surface wind and gust factor are effectively irrelevant (deep benthic habitat). Pressure trend stays in the mix, but six-hour change is minimal—surface fronts damp with depth. Air-temperature weight is zero (water temperature dominates).

Whitefish (siika)

Coregonus lavaretus — European whitefish (several morphs)

Luke — species guide (FI) Luontoportti (FI) FishBase

Feeding Whitefish are planktivores and daytime visual feeders: stomach fullness peaks by day, partly under clear or partly cloudy skies; solid overcast can depress activity (Mookerji et al. 1998; Eckmann et al. 2002). They track diel zooplankton migrations (Mehner et al. 2005).

Temperature Cold-stenothermal: sustained feeding commonly 7–14 °C; above ~18 °C fish drop deep (Wanzenböck et al. 2002). A stable thermocline matters—predictable shoal depth for pelagic feeding bouts.

Reproduction Spawning on bottom or in deep basins depending on form; eggs need cold, oxygenated water. Finland hosts multiple morphs (sparse- vs dense-rakered) with different niches.

WFI highlights Calm water and explicit gust sensitivity (waves disrupt thermocline and shoals). Midday feeding peak (Kahilainen et al. 2004). No lunar term (daytime planktivore). Six-hour pressure signal is weak—no coregonid barometer study found; wide neutral plateau.

Baltic herring (silakka)

Clupea harengus membras — Baltic herring

FishBase Wikipedia (EN) Wikipedia (FI)

Not listed in species_external_links.json (that file mirrors kCommonFavouriteSpeciesIds in preferences_store.dart). FishBase treats the nominal species; the Baltic form is subspecies membras.

Feeding Baltic herring are obligate schoolers: feeding and stomach fullness peak when surface irradiance is low (dusk, overcast) as zooplankton move up and herring follow (Cardinale et al. 2003). Moonlight extends near-surface feeding but less than for zander or burbot because school dynamics track copepod diel migration.

Temperature Recruitment and feeding in the Baltic align with roughly 4–14 °C surface water; feeding stalls below ~2 °C; above ~16 °C stocks often shift farther offshore (Rajasilta 1992; Cardinale et al. 2009).

Reproduction Spring demersal spawning; timing and egg mass depend on food, condition, and environmental noise—year-classes are highly variable in the Baltic.

WFI highlights Calm to light wind (Casini et al. 2006; Finnish trap-net practice). Evening plus post-midnight peaks. Moderate falling pressure during the dusk feeding window (Cardinale et al. 2003). Profile flagged as a calibration candidate blending coastal practice with peer-reviewed behaviour.

Brown trout (taimen)

Salmo trutta — brown trout / sea trout

Luke — species guide (FI) Luontoportti (FI) FishBase

Feeding Brown trout are a cold-water salmonid: thermal preferendum near 12 °C, growth strongest 13–17 °C, feeding fades above ~19 °C (Elliott & Elliott 2010). In rivers and lakes, feeding is strongly dawn- and dusk-peaked with a deep midday lull (Ovidio et al. 1998; Bunnell et al. 2017). Light rain boosts invertebrate drift and terrestrial knock-down—the profile encodes a “light rain” window (Steingrímsson & Gíslason 2002; Bachman 1984).

Reproduction Autumn spawning on gravel in flowing water; lacustrine strains may use littoral zones. Sea-run (“sea trout”) migrations and spawning track flow and temperature regimes.

WFI highlights Precipitation rate is included (Atlantic salmon has no equivalent precipitation trapezoid in the same form). Six-hour pressure change is broad and near-neutral (Stoner 2004 found no strong salmonid signal). Moon and solunar are moderate (Vøllestad et al.).
